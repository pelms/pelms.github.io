   Help With Unicorn Searching.

A brief summary of your options for making Unicorn OPAC and WebCat searches more flexible.

  • Wildcard - The ? sign.
  • Truncation - The $ sign.
  • Stopwords.
  • Operators.
  • Positional Searching.
  • MARC Field Searching.
  • Equivalent Field Searching.
  • Relational Searching.

  Wildcard - The ? sign.

Example
WOM?N will retrieve occurrences of both WOMAN and WOMEN.

Note
? will substitute a single character.
?? will substitute two characters, and so on.

  Truncation - The $ sign.

Example
NURS$ will find NURSE, NURSES and NURSING.

Note
$ will substitute any number of characters (including zero characters).

Variations

Right Hand Truncation
COMPUT$ will find COMPUTER, COMPUTING, COMPUTATION etc

Limited Truncation
MANAGER$1 will find MANAGER and MANAGERS but not MANAGERIAL etc

Embedded Truncation
POL$THENE will find POLYTHENE and POLYCHLORITHENE etc

Left Hand Truncation
$MENT theoretically will find MOVEMENT, PAVEMENT etc but this searching technique is very memory intensive.
Use it with extreme caution.

  Stopwords.

a an as at be but
by by do for if in
is it of on the to

To search stopwords enclose the word in inverted commas.

Example
'A' will retrieve occurrences of the word A.

Note
The stopwords listed above are those currently set on Unicorn. Others may be set if required.

  Operators.

AND
Searches for occurrences of two terms both within the same record.
Example
BRITAIN AND IRELAND retrieves records which contain both BRITAIN and IRELAND.

OR
Searches for occurrences of either one or both of two terms in the same record.
Example
SPAIN OR PORTUGAL retrieves records which contain either SPAIN or PORTUGAL or both.

NOT
Searches for occurrences of the first but not the second term in a single record.

Example
FRANCE NOT PARIS retrieves records which contain FRANCE but not PARIS.

XOR
Searches for occurrences of either the first or the second term in a single record but not both.
Example
USA XOR AMERICA retrieves records which contain either USA or AMERICA but not both.

  Positional Searching.

SAME
Searches for occurrences of two terms both within the same paragraph.
Example
HISTORY SAME GERMANY retrieves records which contain both HISTORY and GERMANY in the same paragraph.
Note
This is the default setting for searching. Typing 'HISTORY GERMANY' would give you the same result as typing 'HISTORY SAME GERMANY'.

WITH
Searches for occurrences of two terms both within the same sentence.
Example
POLITICS WITH POLAND retrieves records which contain both POLITICS and POLAND in the same sentence.

NEAR
Searches for occurrences of two terms adjacent to one another but in any order.
Example
INFORMATION NEAR MANAGEMENT retrieves records which contain the phrases INFORMATION MANAGEMENT and MANAGEMENT INFORMATION.

ADJ
Searches for occurrences of two terms adjacent to one another in the order stated.
Example
INFORMATION ADJ MANAGEMENT retrieves records which contain the phrase INFORMATION MANAGEMENT but will not retrieve records containing the phrase MANAGEMENT INFORMATION.

NESTING
Unicorn will always search what is in ( ) first.
Example
(GERMANY OR AUSTRIA) AND CINEMA
COLD WAR OR (FOREIGN RELATIONS AND (SOVIET UNION OR UNITED STATES))

  MARC Field Searching.


If you happen to know the specific MARC field you wish to search then you can restrict your to just that field by entering your search term followed by the MARC tag enclosed in { }

Example
MACMILLAN {260}
(Searches for a publisher excluding occurrences of the term elsewhere in a record)
HORIZON {440}
(Searches for a television programme in a series entry excluding other occurrences of the term)

  Equivalent Field Searching.


Equivalence searching allows you to search similar MARC fields simultaneously by entering your search term followed by a code enclosed in { }

Example
WORDSWORTH {AU}
(Searches the following MARC fields for your search term - 100, 110, 600, 610, 700, 710)???
CHEMOTHERAPY {TI}
(Searches the following MARC fields for your search term - 240, 245, 440, 745)
ECOLOGY {SU}
(Searches the following MARC fields for your search term - 600, 610, 650, 651, 670, 671)???

  Relational Searching.


Relational searching allows you to use certain operators in order to specify a numerical value or range of values in a certain field. You must enclose the relevant MARC field in { }, note a valid operator and then note a number.
In most cases this technique will be used for restricting your search by publication date.

Example
{260}=1988
(Searches for items published in 1988)

{260}<1956
(Searches for items published before 1956)

{260}>=1992
(Searches for items published either in or after 1992)

Valid Relational Operators

> Greater than
>= Greater than or equal to
= Equal to
<= Less than or equal to
< Less than
